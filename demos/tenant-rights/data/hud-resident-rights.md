# HUD resident rights and responsibilities

Source label: HUD's resident rights and responsibilities guidance.

This is plain-language guidance drawn from public US Department of Housing and
Urban Development (HUD) material on renter rights and the federal Fair Housing
Act. It describes general rights and responsibilities. It does not state the
specific dollar amounts, deadlines, or statute numbers that vary by state and by
lease.

## Decent, safe, and sanitary housing

Renters have the right to live in housing that is decent, safe, and sanitary. A
housing provider is generally responsible for keeping the building and the unit
in good repair, keeping common areas clean and safe, and maintaining the
electrical, plumbing, heating, and other essential systems so the home is fit to
live in. If something essential breaks, the renter should report it to the
landlord or property manager in writing and keep a copy.

## Repairs and maintenance responsibilities

The housing provider is generally responsible for major repairs and for keeping
the unit habitable. The renter is generally responsible for keeping their own
unit clean, using appliances and fixtures properly, and not causing damage
beyond normal wear and tear. Renters should report needed repairs promptly and
in writing. If a serious repair is ignored, a renter can contact their local
housing authority, a local code enforcement office, or a local legal aid
organization to learn about their options. The exact remedies and timelines
depend on state and local law.

## Landlord entry and notice

A renter has a right to reasonable privacy in their home. As a general rule, a
housing provider should give reasonable advance notice before entering an
occupied unit, except in a genuine emergency such as a fire or a burst pipe.
What counts as reasonable notice, and the specific number of hours required, is
set by state and local law and sometimes by the lease, so a renter should check
their lease and their state's rules for the exact requirement.

## Fair housing and discrimination protections

The federal Fair Housing Act makes it illegal to discriminate in housing because
of race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status (such as having
children in the household), or disability. HUD enforces the Act's ban on sex
discrimination to include discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender
identity. These protections apply to renting, to the terms and conditions of a
lease, to advertising, and to most other housing activities. A person who
believes they have experienced housing discrimination can file a complaint with
HUD.

## Reasonable accommodations and modifications

Under fair housing law, renters with disabilities have the right to request a
reasonable accommodation, which is a change to a rule, policy, or service, and a
reasonable modification, which is a physical change to the unit or building, when
it is needed to use and enjoy the home. The housing provider is generally
required to grant reasonable requests. A renter usually makes the request to the
housing provider, who may ask for information that confirms the need when the
disability or the need is not obvious.

## Eviction basics and notice

A renter generally cannot be removed from their home without a proper legal
process. As a general rule, that process includes written notice and, in most
cases, a court proceeding where the renter has a chance to respond. A housing
provider usually cannot force a renter out by changing the locks, removing the
renter's belongings, or shutting off utilities. The specific notice periods, the
allowed reasons, and the steps in the process are set by state and local law, so
a renter facing eviction should get help promptly from local legal aid or a
tenant lawyer.

## Security deposits

A security deposit is money a renter pays at move-in that the housing provider
holds and may use for unpaid rent or for damage beyond normal wear and tear. The
rules for how much can be charged, how the deposit must be held, what can be
deducted, and how quickly it must be returned after move-out are set by state and
local law and by the lease. Because these specifics vary, a renter should check
their lease and their state's rules, or ask local legal aid, for the exact
amounts and deadlines that apply to them.

## Resident rights and responsibilities overview

Renters generally have the right to a home that is decent, safe, and sanitary, to
fair treatment free from illegal discrimination, to reasonable privacy, and to a
proper legal process before an eviction. Renters generally have the
responsibility to pay rent on time, to follow the lease, to keep their unit
reasonably clean and undamaged, to report needed repairs, and to respect their
neighbors. Knowing both sides helps a renter resolve problems and avoid disputes.

## Where to get help

A renter who needs help can contact local legal aid, a tenant lawyer, or their
local HUD office. For housing discrimination, a renter can file a complaint with
HUD. For repair or habitability problems, a local code enforcement office or
local housing authority can explain the local rules. These organizations can give
guidance based on the renter's specific situation and state, which general HUD
material cannot.
